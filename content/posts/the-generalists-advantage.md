I've never fit neatly into a single box. My résumé reads like a list of unrelated obsessions: software engineering, design, writing, philosophy, music production, generative art. For years I treated that as a problem to solve — a sign I needed to *focus*, to pick a lane.

It took me a while to realize it was the opposite. The breadth is the point.

## The myth of depth

The standard career advice goes: pick one thing, get really good at it, repeat. That's good advice for *some* people. But for the curious — the kind of person who reads philosophy on a Sunday and then builds a fractal renderer on a Monday — staying in a single lane is death by a thousand cuts.

## What breadth actually gives you

- **Pattern recognition across domains.** The same idea shows up in music theory, in software architecture, in writing.
- **Boring problems don't kill your momentum.** When the database work gets stale, you can switch to a design problem.
- **You build things specialists can't.** Specialists optimize within a frame. Generalists change the frame.

The polymath, the renaissance mind, the curious generalist — these aren't flaws. They're the actual advantage.
